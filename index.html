The Structure
2 full-body sessions per week. Every session hits every muscle group. 15 sets total each time.
Why full-body and not splits? The "2" in 2-5-15 means each muscle is trained twice a week. A chest/back split would mean chest only gets hit on upper days. Full-body sessions ensure every muscle gets its 2× weekly stimulus — and compound movements mean 3 exercises × 5 sets = 15 sets covers the whole body simultaneously.
2×
Sessions Per Week
Monday and Thursday. Each is a complete full-body workout. ~72 hrs between sessions — enough recovery, frequent enough stimulus.
5
Sets Per Muscle
Each compound movement counts toward 2–3 muscle groups at once. A thruster = 5 sets for quads AND shoulders AND triceps in one go.
15
Sets Per Session
3 exercises × 5 sets = 15. With full-body compounds that's enough to hit every major muscle. Sessions take ~30–35 minutes.
A/B
Alternate Each Week
Session A and B use different exercises for the same muscle groups — same stimulus, different movement pattern. This prevents accommodation over time.

DB Thruster (Squat → Press)
QuadsGlutesShouldersTricepsCore
5 sets8–10 repsNASM Advanced Squat
How: Dumbbells at shoulders. Full squat, then drive through the legs and use that momentum to press overhead in one fluid movement. NASM classifies this as the advanced functional squat pattern. Covers lower body AND shoulders in every rep — the single highest-value movement in this programme.
Deadlift → Upright Row
HamstringsGlutesTrapsShouldersLower Back
5 sets8–10 repsFitness Phantom
How: Hinge into a Romanian deadlift — then as you stand, pull the dumbbells straight up to chin height (upright row). Two movements chained. The hinge fires the entire posterior chain; the row hits traps and side delts. Fitness Phantom lists this as one of the highest muscle-coverage combos available with dumbbells.
DB Floor Press
ChestTricepsShoulders (front)
5 sets8–10 reps
How: Lie flat on the mat, elbows at 45°, lower until triceps touch the floor. The mat limits the range of motion which actually removes shoulder strain — no bench needed. Go heavy. This is your primary push compound, covering chest and triceps for the session.

Back and biceps receive secondary stimulus through stabilisation during the floor press and via the hinge/row movements. For dedicated back volume, swap the Floor Press for the Glute Bridge + Chest Fly from the Swap Library and add a bent-over row as your pull.

Reverse Lunge → Bicep Curl
QuadsGlutesBicepsCore (balance)Hamstrings
5 sets8 reps each legFitness Phantom + NASM Lunge
How: Step back into a reverse lunge — as you drive back up to standing, curl the dumbbells. Reverse lunge (vs forward) is easier on the knee and better activates the glute. The curl happens at the top of the stand, using the natural momentum pause. Covers quads, glutes AND biceps per set.
Single-Leg RDL → Row
HamstringsGlutesBackCore (balance)Biceps
5 sets8 reps each legNASM Advanced Hinge + NML
How: Balance on one leg, hinge forward into a single-leg RDL — at the bottom, row the dumbbell to your hip before returning upright. NASM lists single-leg RDL as the advanced hinge pattern. Adding the row means each rep also hits the back. Go lighter than a standard RDL — balance is the challenge here.
Push-Up → T Rotation
ChestTricepsObliquesShouldersCore
5 sets6–8 reps each sideNASM Advanced Push
How: Do a push-up, then at the top rotate and reach one arm to the ceiling until your body forms a "T". Alternate the rotation side each rep. NASM lists this as their advanced functional push pattern — the rotation adds an oblique and rotational core element completely absent from the floor press in Session A. Load with a dumbbell on your back to increase difficulty.

Shoulders receive secondary stimulus from both the lunge-press and push-up. If you want dedicated shoulder sets, replace the Push-Up → T Rotation with the Arnold Press from the Swap Library (5 sets) — that brings dedicated shoulder volume while still covering triceps.

Core
Optional add-on · Hits every core muscle · 5 sets each · 15 total sets · ~25 min · Rest 60 sec between sets
Why these 3? The ACE EMG study ranked bicycle crunch #1 for oblique activation (248% above a standard crunch) and #1 for rectus abdominis. Dead bug is the gold-standard for the deep transverse abdominis. Plank pass-through is the only movement here that meaningfully loads the serratus anterior and trains pure anti-rotation under dumbbell load. Together they cover every core muscle.

Weighted Dead Bug
Transverse AbsRectus AbdominisHip FlexorsObliquesErector Spinae
5 sets8–10 each sideNASM / ACE Research
How: Lie on your back, hold a dumbbell at chest with both hands, arms extended toward the ceiling. Lower opposite arm and leg toward the floor simultaneously — keep your lower back pressed completely flat the whole time. The DB adds load that forces the deep transverse abdominis to brace far harder than bodyweight alone. The lowering phase is where the work happens — go slow.
Bicycle Crunch
ObliquesRectus AbdominisHip Flexors
5 sets20–25 repsACE Study — Ranked #1
How: Hands lightly behind head, rotate one elbow toward the opposite knee while extending the other leg low and straight — then switch. The ACE landmark EMG study ranked this #1 for both oblique activation (248% above a standard crunch) and rectus abdominis. Reps are higher here because it's bodyweight — go slow and focus on the rotation, not the speed. Don't pull your neck.
DB Plank Pass-Through
Transverse AbsObliquesSerratus AnteriorShouldersHip Flexors
5 sets8–10 each sideNASM Anti-Rotation
How: High plank position with a dumbbell on one side. Reach under with the opposite hand and drag the dumbbell across to the other side, then alternate. The goal is to keep your hips dead still as you move the weight — that anti-rotation resistance is pure oblique and TA work. Set your feet wider than shoulder width for extra stability. Go lighter than you think.

Session C is an optional add-on — fit it in on any non-consecutive day. Wed or Sat work well alongside the Mon/Thu schedule. Rest 60 sec between sets rather than 90 — core exercises recover faster than compound lifts.

Swap Library
Rotate any exercise after 4–6 weeks. Each covers the same muscle groups as what it replaces.
When to swap: After 4–6 weeks your body adapts to the same movement pattern. Swapping in an alternative — same muscle groups, different pattern — restarts the adaptation cycle. You don't need to change everything at once; swapping one exercise per session is enough.

Bulgarian Split Squat
QuadsGlutesHamstringsCore (balance)Calves
3 sets8–10 reps each legNASM Advanced Lunge
How: Place the top of your rear foot on a chair or sofa behind you, front foot stepped well forward. Lower until your front thigh is parallel to the floor — drive through the heel to stand. The elevated rear foot forces your front leg to handle nearly all the load. EMG studies show higher quad and glute activation than a standard squat. Go slow on the descent — that's where the work is.
Pike Push-up
Shoulders (all heads)TricepsUpper ChestCore
3 sets10–12 repsACE / Calisthenics Research
How: Start in a downward dog position — hips high, body in an inverted V. Bend your elbows and lower your head toward the floor between your hands, then press back up. The angle shifts the load heavily onto all three deltoid heads in a way a flat push-up can't reach. Elevate your feet on a chair to increase difficulty and approximate a vertical press.
Superman → Row
Lower BackRear DeltsRhomboidsGlutesTraps
3 sets12–15 repsNASM Posterior Chain
How: Lie face down, arms extended forward. In one motion, lift your chest and arms off the floor — then pull your elbows back toward your hips, squeezing the shoulder blades together at the top. Hold for a beat, extend arms forward again, then lower. The lift fires the entire posterior chain; the row adds rhomboid and rear delt activation. No bicep load without something to pull against — this is pure posterior chain work. Go slow and hold the top position each rep.
Single-Leg Glute Bridge
GlutesHamstringsLower BackCoreHip Flexors
3 sets12 reps each sideNCBI EMG — highest BW glute activation
How: Lie on your back, one foot flat on the floor, the other leg extended straight. Drive through your grounded heel and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from shoulder to knee. Pause and squeeze hard at the top. Single-leg removes the compensation that lets a weak glute hide in a bilateral bridge — EMG studies show significantly higher gluteus maximus activation than the two-leg version.
Known gap — Lats & Biceps: Pure bodyweight training cannot load the lats or biceps without something to pull against. This session covers every other major muscle group. To close the gap: a doorframe row, resistance band, or pull-up bar added as a 6th exercise will complete full-body coverage.
